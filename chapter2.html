1. The Economist as Scientist

Economists approach markets with the same objectivity and careful reasoning used in other sciences. They use the scientific method: observation, theory, and more observation.

Because economists usually cannot run laboratory experiments on entire economies, they rely heavily on historical data from past economic events. They use that data to test whether their theories help explain the real world.

Key idea: Good economic thinking uses evidence. It does not rely only on opinion or personal experience.

2. Assumptions and Models

Assumptions

Assumptions simplify complex reality so economists can focus on the question being studied.

Sticky Prices

Prices can be slow or costly to change. A restaurant, for example, may avoid changing prices often because printing new menus has a cost.

Economic Models

Models are simplified representations of reality. They often use diagrams or equations to make economic relationships easier to see.

3. Economic Model I: The Circular-Flow Diagram

The circular-flow diagram shows how dollars, goods, services, and factors of production move through the economy among households and firms.

The Inner Loop

Households sell factors of production to firms. Firms use those resources to produce goods and services, which are sold back to households.

The Outer Loop

Households spend money, creating revenue for firms. Firms use that revenue to pay households as income in the form of wages, rent, and profit.

Note: Land, labor, and capital are factors of production. Money is not a factor of production; it is the medium that flows in the opposite direction of real resources.

4. Economic Model II: The Production Possibilities Frontier

The Production Possibilities Frontier, or PPF, shows the combinations of output an economy can produce with its available resources and technology.

Efficiency

Points on the frontier represent efficient production. The economy is getting maximum output from its resources.

Inefficiency

Points inside the curve represent inefficient outcomes, often caused by unemployment or unused resources.

Unattainable

Points outside the curve are currently impossible to reach with existing resources and technology.

Because resources are scarce, producing more of one good usually requires producing less of another. This tradeoff is the opportunity cost of production. A PPF is often bowed outward because resources are specialized and not equally suited for producing all goods.

Economic growth shifts the PPF outward when an economy gains better technology, new resources, or improved productive capacity.

Why This Matters

The Production Possibilities Frontier, or PPF, is one of the first major models in economics.

A model is a simplified version of reality. Economists use models to help explain choices, tradeoffs, and limits. The PPF helps us see a basic economic problem: resources are scarce, so every society must decide what to produce.

The PPF shows the different combinations of two goods or services that can be produced when resources are used efficiently.

In this video series, you will learn how the PPF explains:

  • scarcity
  • opportunity cost
  • efficiency
  • underemployment
  • economic growth
  • increasing opportunity cost

In the first video, you will learn how the PPF shows the problem of scarcity.

Scarcity means that we do not have unlimited resources. Because resources are limited, we cannot produce everything we want. Every choice involves a tradeoff.

The PPF shows the maximum possible combinations of two goods that can be produced with available resources and technology.

Key Ideas:

  • A point on the PPF is efficient.
  • A point inside the PPF is inefficient.
  • A point outside the PPF is currently impossible.
  • Moving along the PPF means choosing more of one good and less of another.

In the second video, you will learn what happens when an economy is not using all of its resources.

A point inside the PPF means the economy is producing less than it could. This may happen because workers are unemployed, factories are not being fully used, or resources are being wasted.

The video also explains economic growth. Economic growth happens when an economy can produce more than before. On a PPF graph, this is shown by the frontier shifting outward.

Key Ideas:

  • A point inside the PPF shows underemployment or inefficiency.
  • Moving from inside the PPF to the frontier means the economy is using resources more fully.
  • An outward shift of the PPF shows economic growth.
  • Economic growth can come from more resources, better technology, better education, or improved productivity.

Video 3: Increasing Opportunity Cost

Federal Reserve Education Page:

Open Lesson: Increasing Opportunity Cost
Watch on YouTube

In the third video, you will learn why the PPF is usually curved, or bowed outward.

As an economy produces more of one good, it often has to give up larger and larger amounts of the other good. This is called increasing opportunity cost.

This happens because resources are not equally good at producing everything. Some workers, land, machines, or tools are better suited for one type of production than another.

Key Ideas:

  • Opportunity cost is what you give up when you make a choice.
  • Increasing opportunity cost means that the more you produce of one good, the more of the other good you must give up.
  • The bowed-out shape of the PPF shows increasing opportunity cost.
  • Resources are specialized, so shifting production is not always easy.

Watch For:

As you watch, pay attention to why the tradeoff gets larger as production changes.

  • Why does opportunity cost increase?
  • Why are some resources better at producing one good than another?
  • Why is the PPF usually bowed outward instead of a straight line?
Big Takeaway

The PPF is a simple graph, but it teaches several major ideas in economics.

It shows that scarcity forces choices. Choices create tradeoffs. Tradeoffs create opportunity costs. Economies are efficient when they use their resources fully, but they can also experience inefficiency, underemployment, and growth.

Understanding the PPF will help you understand many later topics in economics, including production, specialization, trade, economic growth, and decision-making.

5. Microeconomics vs. Macroeconomics

Field Focus Examples
Microeconomics How households and firms make decisions and interact in specific markets. Household choices, firm behavior, or a tax on one industry.
Macroeconomics Economy-wide phenomena and the overall performance of the economy. Inflation, unemployment, federal deficits, and growth policies.

6. Positive vs. Normative Analysis

Positive Statements

Positive statements describe how the world is. They can be tested or confirmed with evidence.

Example: Money growth causes inflation.

Normative Statements

Normative statements describe what ought to be. They depend on values, ethics, politics, or personal judgment.

Example: The government ought to reduce inflation.

7. Why Economists Disagree

Economists may disagree because they make different scientific judgments about theories and data. They may also disagree because public policy involves values and tradeoffs.

Even though public debates can make economists seem divided, there is broad agreement on many basic principles of economics.

Big Takeaway

Economists think like scientists by using models, assumptions, and evidence. They study choices under scarcity, the flow of resources and money, the tradeoffs shown by the Production Possibilities Frontier, and the difference between factual analysis and value-based policy advice.

Key Ideas

  • Every choice has an opportunity cost.
  • Tradeoffs happen because resources have alternative uses.
  • The production possibilities frontier shows combinations an economy can produce.
  • Points inside the PPF represent inefficient production.
  • Economic growth can shift the PPF outward.
