I. Introduction to Economic Science

Economics is not just about money. It is about choices. Economics is the study of how society manages its scarce resources. Economists study how individuals decide what to buy, how much to work, how businesses and households interact in markets, and how the economy as a whole grows or struggles.

The central idea is scarcity. Scarcity means that people have unlimited wants, but society has limited resources. Because we cannot have everything, we must make choices.

Big Idea: Every economic decision begins with scarcity. If resources were unlimited, people would not need to make trade-offs.

Economics Happens at the Margin

Economists often ask what changes when a person does one more thing: one more hour of studying, one more slice of pizza, one more shift at work, or one more dollar spent. The key question is whether the extra benefit is greater than the extra cost.

II. Ten Principles at a Glance

How People Make Decisions

Principles 1-4: Individuals face trade-offs, compare costs and benefits, think at the margin, and respond to incentives.

How People Interact

Principles 5-7: Trade can make people better off, markets usually organize activity well, and governments can sometimes improve outcomes.

How the Economy Works as a Whole

Principles 8-10: Living standards depend on productivity, too much money can cause inflation, and society may face short-run trade-offs.

III. Category One: How People Make Decisions

Principle 1: People Face Trade-offs

To get one thing we like, we usually have to give up another thing we like.

Simple example: If a school spends more money on new athletic fields, it may have less money for new classroom technology.

Principle 2: The Cost of Something Is What You Give Up to Get It

The real cost of a choice is not only the money spent. It is also the next best option you gave up. Economists call this opportunity cost.

Simple example: If you spend Saturday working a job, the opportunity cost may be time with friends or time to study.

Principle 3: Rational People Think at the Margin

Rational people make decisions by comparing the extra benefit and extra cost of one more action. Economists call these marginal changes.

Simple example: Should you study one more hour? The answer depends on whether the extra grade improvement is worth the extra hour.

Principle 4: People Respond to Incentives

An incentive is something that encourages or discourages a person from acting. Rewards, punishments, prices, grades, taxes, and discounts can all change behavior.

Simple example: If a store offers 50% off, more people may buy the product.

IV. Category Two: How People Interact

Principle 5: Trade Can Make Everyone Better Off

Trade is not automatically a contest where one person wins and another loses. When trade is voluntary, both sides expect to benefit.

Simple example: A student buys lunch from the cafeteria because the food is worth more to her than the money. The cafeteria sells lunch because the money is worth more than keeping the food.

Principle 6: Markets Are Usually a Good Way to Organize Economic Activity

In a market economy, households and firms make decisions through buying and selling. Prices act like signals that help people decide what to produce, what to buy, and how much to sell.

Simple example: If many people want a product, its price may rise. That higher price tells producers there is demand.

Principle 7: Governments Can Sometimes Improve Market Outcomes

Markets need rules. Government helps protect property rights, enforce contracts, and sometimes correct problems when markets fail.

Simple example: Pollution is a market failure because the cost falls on people who may not be part of the original transaction.

V. Category Three: How the Economy Works as a Whole

Principle 8: A Country's Standard of Living Depends on Its Ability to Produce Goods and Services

The more productive a country is, the more goods and services it can provide. Productivity is one of the most important causes of living standards.

Simple example: Better tools, education, technology, and training can help workers produce more per hour.

Principle 9: Prices Rise When the Government Prints Too Much Money

Inflation means the overall level of prices is rising. One major cause of serious inflation is rapid growth in the amount of money in the economy.

Simple example: If there is much more money chasing the same amount of goods, prices tend to rise.

Principle 10: Society Faces a Short-Run Trade-off Between Inflation and Unemployment

In the short run, policies that increase spending may reduce unemployment, but they can also increase inflation. This trade-off is temporary and complicated.

Simple example: If people spend more, businesses may hire more workers. But if demand rises too quickly, prices may also rise.

VI. Glossary of Essential Terms

Term Definition
ScarcityLimited resources and unlimited wants.
EconomicsThe study of how society manages its scarce resources.
EfficiencyGetting the most benefit from scarce resources.
EqualityDistributing economic prosperity more evenly among society's members.
Opportunity CostWhatever must be given up to get something else.
RationalActing purposefully to achieve a goal.
Marginal ChangeA small adjustment to an existing plan.
IncentiveSomething that encourages or discourages action.
Market EconomyAn economy where households and firms make decisions through markets.
Property RightsThe ability to own and control scarce resources.
Invisible HandThe idea that self-interested choices can sometimes lead to good social outcomes.
Market FailureA situation where a market does not allocate resources efficiently.
ExternalityA side effect of an action that affects a bystander.
Market PowerThe ability of one person or group to strongly influence prices.
ProductivityThe amount of goods and services produced from each unit of labor.
InflationAn increase in the overall level of prices.
Business CycleFluctuations in economic activity over time.

VII. Critical Synthesis: Trade Is Not Zero-Sum

A common mistake is to think that trade is always a zero-sum game, meaning one person's gain must be another person's loss. Economics teaches a different idea: voluntary trade can make both sides better off.

  • Voluntary exchange means both sides expect to benefit.
  • Specialization allows people and nations to focus on what they do well.
  • Trade allows people to consume more variety at lower cost.
  • Restrictions on trade can reduce the total gains available to society.

AP Micro Connection: Later in the course, we will use graphs such as the Production Possibilities Frontier, or PPF, to show trade-offs, opportunity cost, specialization, and gains from trade.

Key Ideas

  • Scarcity means people cannot have everything they want.
  • Economics studies choices under constraints.
  • Incentives influence behavior and decision-making.
  • Marginal thinking compares additional benefits and additional costs.
  • Economic models simplify reality so we can analyze choices more clearly.
