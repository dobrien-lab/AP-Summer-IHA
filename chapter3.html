Chapter 3

Comparative Advantage and Gains from Trade

Learn why specialization and voluntary exchange can make people and nations better off.

Essential Question

Why can trade benefit both sides even when one side is better at producing everything?

Required Reading

Read the Absolute Advantage vs. Comparative Advantage guide below. Focus on the difference between producing more and producing at a lower opportunity cost.

Required Reading

Absolute Advantage vs. Comparative Advantage

Trade works because people, businesses, and countries do not all have to produce everything for themselves. They can specialize, trade with others, and end up with more than they could have produced alone.

Big Idea

Economists call this interdependence: relying on others through trade. The key question is not only who can produce more. The deeper question is who gives up less when producing something.

1. Absolute Advantage: Who Can Produce More?

Absolute advantage is the easiest idea in economics. It simply means: which side can produce more?

A person, business, or country has an absolute advantage if it can produce more of a good using the same amount of resources, time, or labor.

Simple rule: Ask, who makes more? That side has the absolute advantage.

Example: If Japan can make 6 units of steel per hour and Korea can make 8 units of steel per hour, then Korea has the absolute advantage in steel because Korea produces more steel per hour.

2. Comparative Advantage: Who Can Do It at the Lowest Cost?

Comparative advantage means: which side can produce something at the lowest opportunity cost?

Opportunity cost means what you give up when you choose one option instead of another.

Simple rule: Ask, who gives up less? That side has the comparative advantage.

Comparative advantage is not about who is faster, stronger, or more productive overall. It is about who sacrifices less when producing something.

3. Why These Ideas Are Different

Absolute Advantage Asks

Who produces more?

Comparative Advantage Asks

Who gives up less?

The Trade Lesson

A country can produce more of something but still not have the lowest opportunity cost.

4. The Tiger Woods Example

Tiger Woods might be better than most people at both golf and mowing his lawn. That means he may have an absolute advantage in both activities.

Activity Who May Be Better?
Playing golfTiger Woods
Mowing the lawnTiger Woods

But should Tiger Woods mow his own lawn? Probably not. If Tiger spends time mowing the lawn, he gives up time he could have used to earn money playing golf. A lawn service gives up much less by mowing the lawn.

Even if Tiger is better at mowing, the lawn service has the comparative advantage in lawn mowing because it can do the job at a lower opportunity cost.

To find comparative advantage, calculate opportunity cost. Ask: what does each country give up to produce one more unit of this good?

7. Who Has Comparative Advantage?

Televisions

Japan gives up 2 steel to make 1 television. Korea gives up 4 steel.

Result: Japan has the comparative advantage in televisions.

Steel

Japan gives up 1/2 television to make 1 steel. Korea gives up 1/4 television.

Result: Korea has the comparative advantage in steel.

8. The Big Trade Lesson

Countries should specialize according to comparative advantage, not absolute advantage.

Country Should Specialize In
JapanTelevisions
KoreaSteel

Each country is producing the good it can make at the lowest opportunity cost. When countries specialize and trade, total production can rise, and both countries can end up better off.

10. Final Student Takeaway

Absolute advantage is about production power: who can produce more?

Comparative advantage is about opportunity cost: who can produce it for the lowest cost?

Trade is based on comparative advantage because even a less productive country can still benefit by specializing in the good it produces at the lowest opportunity cost.

Key Ideas

  • Absolute advantage compares productivity.
  • Comparative advantage compares opportunity cost.
  • Specialization allows people to focus on lower opportunity cost production.
  • Voluntary trade can create mutual gains.
  • Terms of trade determine whether both sides benefit from exchange.
