Welcome to AP Microeconomics

AP Microeconomics is a college-level course, which means we will move quickly, use precise vocabulary, and practice applying concepts to real-world situations. The goal of this summer assignment is not simply to get work done. The goal is to help you arrive in September already familiar with the basic language and thinking style of economics.

Economics is the study of choices. It asks how people, businesses, and societies make decisions when resources are limited. This course is about trade-offs, incentives, markets, decisions, and consequences.

Your AP Microeconomics Summer Notebook must be on paper. You may use a notebook or binder, but it should not be a Google Doc or digital notebook.

Why paper? Graphing, labeling, drafting, and revising diagrams are still paper-based skills in AP Economics. You need practice drawing models by hand.

Summer Assignment Overview

Each chapter includes:

  1. Reading the student-facing guide on this website.
  2. Completing the chapter Google Form.
  3. Building detailed notebook notes.
  4. Studying and using the vocabulary.
  5. Preparing to explain the concepts out loud in normal language.

The goal is to become conversant in the vocabulary. You should be able to explain terms, give examples, and recognize how ideas work in real life.

AP Microeconomics Summer Notebook

Your notebook must be on paper. Use a physical notebook or binder organized by chapter. It should show evidence that you worked carefully over the summer.

Graphing and drafting are still paper-based skills in AP Economics. You will need to draw, label, revise, and explain models by hand throughout the course.

Each Chapter Section

Include the chapter title, key vocabulary, main ideas, visuals, and application examples.

Vocabulary

For each term, include a definition, example, and why it matters.

Required Visuals

Include a circular-flow diagram, a PPF, and a basic supply and demand graph.

Notebook Requirements by Chapter

  • Chapter 1: At least 10 key terms and one real-life economic decision.
  • Chapter 2: At least 10 key terms, a circular-flow diagram, a PPF, efficient/inefficient/impossible points, economic growth, and positive/normative examples.
  • Chapter 3: At least 10 key terms and an example showing why someone better at everything might still specialize.
  • Chapter 4: At least 10 key terms, a supply and demand graph, equilibrium, demand and supply shifts, shortage, and surplus.
