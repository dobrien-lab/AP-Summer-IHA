Supply and Demand: How Markets Work

Supply and demand is one of the most important models in economics. It explains why prices rise and fall, why shortages happen, why stores sometimes have too much inventory, and how buyers and sellers organize scarce resources through markets.

Essential Question

1. Markets and Competitive Markets

A market is a group of buyers and sellers of a particular good or service. A market does not have to be a physical place; markets can exist in stores, online, in neighborhoods, across the country, or around the world.

Market

Where buyers and sellers interact.

Competitive Market

A market with many buyers and many sellers, where no single buyer or seller controls the price.

Price Taker

A buyer or seller who must accept the market price.

A monopoly is the opposite case: a market with only one seller. In a monopoly, the seller has much more control over price because buyers do not have many alternatives.

2. Demand

Demand shows the behavior of buyers. Quantity demanded is the amount of a good that buyers are willing and able to purchase at a specific price.

Law of Demand: When price rises, quantity demanded usually falls. When price falls, quantity demanded usually rises.

Demand curves usually slope downward because price and quantity demanded move in opposite directions. This is called an inverse relationship.

3. Demand Shifters

A change in demand means the entire demand curve shifts. This is caused by something other than the price of the good itself.

Demand Shifter What It Means
IncomeConsumers have more or less money to spend.
Price of SubstitutesThe price of a rival good changes.
Price of ComplementsThe price of a related good changes.
Tastes and PreferencesConsumers want the good more or less.
ExpectationsConsumers expect future prices or income to change.
Number of BuyersMore or fewer consumers are in the market.

Important rule: The price of the good itself does not shift demand.

4. Normal Goods, Inferior Goods, Substitutes, and Complements

Normal Good

A good people buy more of when income rises, such as restaurant meals, vacations, or name-brand products.

Inferior Good

A good people may buy less of when income rises, such as instant noodles, used clothing, or bus rides.

Substitutes

Goods that can replace each other. If Pepsi becomes more expensive, demand for Coke may rise.

Complements

Goods used together. If gasoline becomes more expensive, demand for large SUVs may fall.

5. Movement Along Demand vs. Shift in Demand

Change in Quantity Demanded

A movement along the same demand curve caused only by a change in the price of the good itself.

Change in Demand

A shift of the entire demand curve caused by a demand shifter, such as income, tastes, or the price of a substitute.

Memory rule: Price changes quantity demanded. Everything else shifts demand.

6. Supply

Supply shows the behavior of sellers. Quantity supplied is the amount of a good that sellers are willing and able to sell at a specific price.

Law of Supply: When price rises, quantity supplied usually rises. When price falls, quantity supplied usually falls.

Supply curves usually slope upward because price and quantity supplied move in the same direction. This is called a direct relationship.

7. Supply Shifters

A change in supply means the entire supply curve shifts. This is caused by something other than the price of the good itself.

Supply Shifter What It Means
Input PricesThe cost of resources used to make the good changes.
TechnologyTools or methods make production easier, faster, or cheaper.
ExpectationsProducers expect future prices to change.
Number of SellersMore or fewer firms are in the market.

Memory rule: Price changes quantity supplied. Everything else shifts supply.

8. Equilibrium, Surplus, and Shortage

Equilibrium

The point where supply and demand intersect. Quantity supplied equals quantity demanded.

Surplus

Quantity supplied is greater than quantity demanded. This usually happens when price is too high.

Shortage

Quantity demanded is greater than quantity supplied. This usually happens when price is too low.

Law of Supply and Demand: Prices naturally adjust to bring quantity supplied and quantity demanded into balance.

9. How to Analyze Market Changes

  1. Decide which curve shifts: does the event affect buyers or sellers?
  2. Decide the direction of the shift: does the curve increase or decrease?
  3. Find the new equilibrium: what happens to equilibrium price and quantity?

AP Micro Magic Sentence

Because [event] affects [buyers/sellers], [demand/supply] shifts [left/right], causing equilibrium price to [rise/fall] and equilibrium quantity to [rise/fall].

10. When Both Curves Shift

Sometimes both supply and demand shift at the same time. When this happens, one result may be clear, while the other may be uncertain.

  • If both shifts push price in the same direction, price is clear, but quantity may be ambiguous.
  • If both shifts push quantity in the same direction, quantity is clear, but price may be ambiguous.

11. Why Prices Matter

Prices are signals. They help guide the decisions of buyers and sellers by helping markets decide what should be produced, how much should be produced, who gets the goods, and where labor and resources should go.

In a market economy, prices help coordinate millions of decisions without a central planner.

Common Student Mistakes

Saying Price Shifts Demand

Price causes movement along the curve, not a shift of the curve.

Confusing Supply and Quantity Supplied

If the price rises, quantity supplied increases. The supply curve itself does not shift.

Forgetting Complements Move Together

If the price of gasoline rises, demand for large SUVs may fall.

Forgetting Substitutes Move Opposite

If the price of Pepsi rises, demand for Coke may rise.

Final Student Takeaway

Supply and demand explain how markets work. Demand shows the behavior of buyers. Supply shows the behavior of sellers. Equilibrium shows where buyers and sellers agree.

The most important skill is knowing the difference between a movement along a curve and a shift of the whole curve.

Remember: price changes quantity demanded or quantity supplied. Non-price factors shift the whole demand or supply curve.

Key Ideas

  • Demand shows the quantities buyers are willing and able to purchase at different prices.
  • Supply shows the quantities sellers are willing and able to produce at different prices.
  • Market equilibrium occurs where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.
  • A shortage occurs when quantity demanded is greater than quantity supplied.
  • A surplus occurs when quantity supplied is greater than quantity demanded.
  • Changes in non-price factors can shift demand or supply.
